"What's our AI visibility like?"

If my CEO or CMO asked me that, here's what I'd do.

First, run our brand through HubSpot's AEO Grader. Then summarize what stands out.

I did this for SparkToro.

The report correctly identified us as a niche audience research tool. And it picked up on one of our biggest advantages: credibility with marketing practitioners. That's affirming because it confirms what I already suspected.

Other parts need a human read:

The competitive set is messy. SparkToro gets compared to Semrush, Ahrefs, Similarweb, Brandwatch, and more. Some are fair nearby alternatives. They're not all direct competitors. But this info is useful — because it shows how AI is categorizing the brand, even when it gets that wrong. And if AI believes something off about you, well… odds are part of your audience does too.

The share of voice info requires nuance. SparkToro shows up at 24% in ChatGPT and Perplexity, but just 12.5% in Gemini — where Semrush and Similarweb both rank higher. Our prospects might currently believe we all have the same offerings even though we don't.

The growth areas were plausible but generic: more enterprise features, more integrations, more automation. Useful? Sure. But you could advise that for most SaaS companies.

So here's what I'd do next:

1/ Find what's accurate.
What is AI already getting right? For us: audience research, credibility among marketers, ease of use and affordability, unique insights, and credible data sources.

2/ Find the misconceptions.
Where is AI putting us in the wrong category, comparing us to the wrong tools, or understating what we do?

3/ Turn the gaps into priorities.
That's a to-do list for website copy, content, PR, sales enablement, and category education.

Then run this analysis again in 1-2 months to see what moved.

"B2B buyers are on LinkedIn" is true. It's also unsurprising.

I ran an audience research report for mid-market RevOps leaders at B2B SaaS companies, and as you might've guessed, 80% of this audience uses LinkedIn. So should LinkedIn be in the plan? Of course.

But the more useful question is: "Where does this audience find ideas they trust enough to repeat?"

RevOps leaders also over-index on Slack — 201% compared with the US average. But it isn't reasonable to like... go build a Slack marketing strategy.

More likely, they're using Slack because it's their company chat tool. This signals they likely work in fast-moving, cross-functional environments where internal conversations shape decisions. Unfortunately, you can't request an invite to their company Slack and start doing your marketing there (though if you can finagle that, I'd love to hear all about it).

What you can do is think about creating things worth bringing into those internal Slack conversations. The asset you create should not just be "clickable." It should be forwardable.

Something a VP of Revenue can drop into Slack with:
"This is exactly what I've been trying to explain."
-or-
"Can we talk about this in our team meeting?"

That could be a:

- Benchmark report
- Calculator or diagnostic
- Spicy-but-useful point of view
- Teardown of a common RevOps mistake
- Comparison guide that makes an internal debate easier

LinkedIn belongs in the plan. But it's not the whole strategy.
