How did my beloved em dash become so damned divisive?

The AI-writing pushback is very… human.

In 2025 the em dash (aka this humble punctuation mark "—") became the smoking gun that "proves" someone used AI to write their LinkedIn post.

Now writers have been using em dashes for centuries. Dickens. Shakespeare. Morrison. They all love a good em dash. Em dashes are especially popular in online writing. I've been creating content consistently since 2019, and I bet 50% of my posts and newsletters include an em dash. (Sometimes I get a little too excited and throw those bad boys around like confetti.)

But nobody gave a damn about em dashes until we started obsessing over "AI tells". Now suddenly, they're everywhere… and they make people MAD.

—

So the real question: Why does spotting an em dash—and assuming it's AI—make people so bloody angry?

I have 3 theories, backed by behavioral science:

**1. Labor Illusion**
We place higher value on things we believe required human effort. When you spot what you think is an AI "tell," you feel tricked. Content that seemed thoughtful now feels cheap.

**2. Authenticity Violation**
We expect LinkedIn posts to be a window into someone's mind and experience. Thinking a post is AI-generated triggers a sense of betrayal, as if we were duped into believing something fake was real.

**3. Effort Justification & Cognitive Dissonance**
If you've just spent hours crafting your own post, someone using AI shortcuts feels deeply unfair. To resolve that psychological discomfort ("Why did I even bother?"), we lash out at the perceived 'cheater'.

So yeah, when you think about it this way... the em dash rage makes perfect psychological sense.

—

I believe that all this em dash backlash is really something much deeper—we're afraid that authentic human expression is becoming extinct, and we're desperately trying to maintain control. But authenticity is about so much more than your writing process.

It's determined by whether your ideas resonate, whether your perspective adds value, and whether your unique voice comes through.

So let's stop policing punctuation and start celebrating good ideas—regardless of how they were crafted.

Agreed?

Solving a problem gets you clients.
Naming it first can build an empire (worth $21M+)…

Chris Walker went from a lone freelancer to building one of the fastest growing demand gen agencies in history. I remember watching him explode on LinkedIn—I'd never seen anything like it. And just 6 years in, he sold his agency for life-changing money.

How'd he do it?

By naming something B2B marketers were already experiencing: that maddening gap between where your attribution software said you were getting leads and where they're actually coming from.

He called it "The Measurement Gap." That was the problem.

Then he came in hot with the named solution: those invisible conversations where buying actually happens—the podcast mentions, the LinkedIn threads, the Slack DMs—were "Dark Social."

Suddenly, every frustrated marketer had words for their frustration. And Chris's demand agency sold a solution to get clients onto those "dark" channels other agencies ignored.

Behavioral scientists call this the "Rumpelstiltskin Effect." Once a problem has a name, people feel power over it.

Before the name: "We're getting leads but still can't scale."
After the name: "We're probably pouring money into the wrong channels because of the Measurement Gap. But if we invest more in content for dark social, our budget will go further."

And Chris? He went from 3 freelance clients to the "Father of Modern Demand Gen." Maybe there were "better" demand gen agencies. But they weren't nearly as memorable.

That's the real lesson.

Most experts are solving problems their buyers can't even articulate yet. They're delivering answers to questions their clients haven't learned to ask.

Coin the problem first. Then sell the solution they need to solve it.

I know there's that… *thing* that annoys you and your customers. What are you gonna name it?
